For developers working in Northern Virginia, site plan approval is where a project moves from concept to reality. It is also where projects frequently stall, sometimes for months, due to procedural missteps, incomplete submissions, or unanticipated opposition.

Unlike a simple by-right building permit, site plan review is a discretionary process in most Northern Virginia jurisdictions. The reviewing body — typically the planning commission or, in some cases, the board of supervisors — evaluates not only compliance with the zoning ordinance but also issues like traffic impact, stormwater management, tree preservation, architectural compatibility, and adequacy of public facilities.

## The Key Jurisdictional Differences

Northern Virginia is not monolithic. Each county and independent city has its own site plan ordinance, its own review procedures, and its own staff culture. What works in Fairfax County may not work in Arlington, and the City of Alexandria has its own distinct process shaped by its historic preservation overlay and small-area plans.

In Fairfax County, for example, most site plan applications go through an extensive staff review process before reaching the Planning Commission. The county requires pre-application conferences and encourages early engagement with residents — steps that are effectively mandatory for any project of meaningful scale.

Arlington County operates under a somewhat different model, with site plans often tied to General Land Use Plan amendments and frequently subject to conditions negotiated through the county's civic association review process.

## Common Pitfalls

The most frequent causes of site plan delays include inadequate transportation impact analyses, failure to coordinate with utility providers early enough in the process, and underestimating the influence of community input during the public hearing stage. In jurisdictions like Fairfax County and Arlington, organized civic association opposition can significantly alter the timeline and, in some cases, the outcome of an application.

Another common issue is the relationship between proffers and site plan conditions. In Virginia, proffers are voluntary commitments made during the rezoning stage, but they become binding obligations that carry forward into the site plan. Developers sometimes discover at the site plan stage that proffers made during rezoning create design constraints they did not fully anticipate.

## When to Engage Land Use Counsel

The ideal time to bring in a land use attorney is before the first pre-application meeting — not after a denial or a round of unfavorable staff comments. Early legal involvement ensures that the application is structured to meet the jurisdiction's specific requirements, that potential zoning issues are identified before they become obstacles, and that community engagement is handled strategically.

If a site plan application has already been denied or subjected to conditions that make the project infeasible, there are avenues for appeal or resubmission, but the options and timelines vary by jurisdiction.
